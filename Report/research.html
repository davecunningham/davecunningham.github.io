Chapter 01
The problem — what the system was forcing clinicians to become
Context

ND services are under profound strain — and documentation is at the heart of it

Through pathway mapping, clinician interviews and admin observation, three specific pain points emerged that AI scribe technology was designed to address directly. These aren't small inefficiencies. They were changing the nature of clinical work — and taking something real from families in the room.

Pain point 01

Documentation was eating assessments alive

In a one-hour assessment, clinicians were expected to simultaneously introduce themselves, conduct risk screening, observe the child, ask parents structured questions — and record everything live, across multiple systems. Documentation didn't happen after the session. It competed with the session itself.

"You've got a lot to do… and you've got to make sure you record those in all the relevant different places."

— Clinician, pathway research
What changed

Clinicians could finally just be present

With the AI scribe running in the background, clinicians stopped typing and started listening. What had been a divided, fragmented experience — watching a child while simultaneously logging notes — became something closer to a real conversation. This wasn't a marginal improvement. Clinicians described it as transformative.

"I feel so much more engaged with the family and it feels more like a conversation than an assessment."

— Michaela, clinician, first week of pilot

"I can delve deeper into some things as it flows so much better."

— Sinead, clinician
72–80% time saved on assessment write-up
Pain point 02

Reports piled up for days — and writing from memory was dangerous

Back-to-back sessions with no protected documentation time meant clinicians reconstructed clinical notes days later, from memory. A typical new patient assessment: 15 minutes reading the referral, 1.5 hours in session, and 1.5–2 hours writing the report. Complex cases ran to 5 or 6 hours. Reconstructing detail from memory wasn't just inefficient — it introduced safeguarding risk.

"I'm normally in the session until 2.45 and then don't get to do the write-up until my admin day — two days later — which can take 1–2 hours to write and proofread."

— Sinead, clinician, before the pilot
What changed

Near-final reports in minutes, not days

The draft appeared while the clinician was still in the building. The gap between session and write-up — which had grown to days — collapsed to minutes. Backlogs cleared. Extra clinic slots became possible. For the first time, clinicians described being fully up to date with their work.

"The family were sat down at 1.35 and the report draft was on my screen by 2.38."

— Sinead, clinician, first day using Magic Notes

"This is the first time in many years I've been fully up to date with my reports. It feels great."

— Sarah, clinician
~124 minutes saved per assessment write-up
Pain point 03

Report writing was exhausting — and it showed in how clinicians wrote

Without adequate support, clinicians developed defensive habits: verbose reports covering every angle, long caveats to manage parental expectation, reluctance to shorten anything in case it mattered later. For neurodivergent clinicians in particular, the pressure to produce detailed, "safe" reports created a specific kind of cognitive burden that no one was talking about.

"The head spin of details and perfectionism is annoying as heck — but now MN writes such lovely reports and it's like: that sounds great."

— Sarah, clinician (who is autistic)
What changed

Reports became proportionate — and readable for families

AI-generated drafts in neuro-affirming language came back consistently well-written. Clinicians spent time shaping and reviewing rather than generating from scratch. Reports shrank from 11 pages to 5. Families received clearer, more useful documents. And the tool found uses nobody anticipated — including real-time translation into Polish and Tamil.

"I also felt the need to get every single detail in and I've managed to go from 11 pages to 5! Which may still be a lot but much better than I was doing before!"

— Penny, clinician

"It's taken so much stress out of report writing and I enjoy reading them again."

— Sarah, clinician

"It translated my whole report into Polish as an additional copy for the parents who didn't have any English."

— Tasha, clinician (bilingual session)
149 "improved" quality ratings. 0 "worse".
