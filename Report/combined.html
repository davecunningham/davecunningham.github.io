02 What changed for clinicians and families
Human impact

Behind the numbers

The time savings are real. But the more important finding from both pilots is what happened inside appointments — for clinicians, for children and adults, and for their families.

Clinicians could be fully present

Both sites described the same shift: without the pressure of simultaneous note-taking, clinicians could watch more, listen more, and connect more naturally with patients and families.

I played Connect 4 for an entire session… and I was still able to ask Mum questions and really engage with the child. I could see how he communicated, how he focused, how he coped with stress, how he took turns… Before Magic Notes, my face would have been in the computer and I wouldn't have seen any of that. Clinician · CTM
You can focus with the kids… I can just focus on the play, and it's not broken up every time I've got to write something down. Clinician B · Children's Autism team · Hywel Dda

A safeguarding response within the hour

During an assessment in Hywel Dda, a clinician uncovered a serious safeguarding concern. Without the AI scribe, producing the written record to act would have taken hours or days. Magic Notes generated the report immediately after the session. Services were able to act within the hour.

Magic Notes allowed that to happen — I wouldn't have been able to send anything that day. Clinician B · Specialist Practitioner in ASD · Hywel Dda

Reports done before leaving the room

Both sites described the same shift from lengthy post-session write-ups to reports nearly complete before the clinician left the room. Clinicians stopped working evenings and weekends to clear documentation.

"Magic Notes gave me back the part of my job I love."

Clinician · CTM

"I think probably a month ago I was thinking of early retirement because I was thinking I'm not as fast as I used to be… but I think now… I'll get through this because I've got a tool that's gonna speed me up enough to do that."

Clinician A · Adult ADHD Clinical Nurse Specialist · Hywel Dda

Less stress, less mental load

It settles your nervous system because you know the information is there. Clinician A · Adult ADHD Clinical Nurse Specialist · Hywel Dda
Some of my appointments now finish within an hour. I've then got time to reflect… time to make a cup of tea. I can't tell you how great it is. Clinician · CTM
I feel a relief that it's capturing everything. Clinician B · Children's Autism team · Hywel Dda
The bulk of admin work… is saved. The anxiety over doing the admin is less. We're better clinicians. Clinician · CTM

Families and patients noticed too

That was brilliant. She hasn't opened up to anybody like that in years. Parent · CTM · following their child's session
Parents felt heard when they knew their comments were being recorded and written up alongside the assessment. Clinician · Children's Autism team · Hywel Dda
Consent was never a problem. In both CTM and Hywel Dda, no family refused consent during either pilot. In several cases, families actively welcomed the recording — reassured that their concerns were being captured accurately rather than filtered or forgotten.
03 What we learned
Lessons from 11 weeks

What the pilots tell us

Where one pilot might be coincidence, two pilots in different health boards with different teams tell us something more reliable.

Finding 01

The time savings are real and consistent

The savings seen in CTM were replicated in Hywel Dda and in some task types exceeded them. The exception — panel case presentation — reflected incomplete template configuration, not a failure of the tool.

Finding 02

It works across different clinical cultures

CTM and Hywel Dda have different team structures, clinical cultures, and workflow patterns. The tool worked consistently across both. Benefits are not site-specific.

Finding 03

Quality consistently improved — or held steady

Across 400+ sessions between the two pilots, the tool was never recorded as making quality worse. This finding is consistent across every task type and every clinician.

Finding 04

The human impact is the same everywhere

The qualitative stories from CTM and Hywel Dda mirror each other almost perfectly: clinicians more present, patients more engaged, families more heard.

Finding 05

There are hundreds of small savings to find

Every template refinement, every better prompt, shaves minutes off a task. One five-minute saving per task might not register in a table — but across a team, across a year, those savings add up to weeks of clinical time. Supporting clinicians to notice and share these micro-improvements is one of the highest-return investments a service can make.

Finding 06

Adoption requires active support

In both pilots, clinicians who trusted the tool early and stopped double-documenting benefited most. Support for early adoption — peer tips, example prompts, reassurance — is essential.

04 What we learned about making it work
Lessons learned

Making templates brilliant, not just good

Both pilots showed that the first version of a template can be good. But iterating it — with real clinicians, in real sessions — can make it brilliant.

In the adult ADHD team, template improvements directly drove the leap to 4.9/5 ease-of-use scores. In the children's Autism team, templates were still being refined throughout the pilot — and clinicians could already feel the difference each iteration made.

The returns on template investment are asymmetric. Spending 30 minutes refining a template used 50 times a week can save 30 hours of clinical time. Every small improvement compounds.

Since they've changed the template, it flows much nicer… The new template is really good. I gotta be honest. It's great. Clinician · CTM
The implication: Build time for template iteration into the plan. Make it easy for clinicians to flag what to change. Treat each refinement as genuinely valuable — because it is.

Other key lessons

Prompts matter

Knowing how to ask the right questions

Prompts are a core part of the workflow, not an optional extra. Services need to plan for prompt patterns and share them — not assume people will discover them independently.

Clinical impact, not just admin

The benefits go beyond saving time

The safeguarding story and the "extra patient" story both show that the effects of AI scribing are directly clinical. Frame the case around clinical outcomes, not just staff time.

Organisation first

Sort out access and governance before you start

The biggest early risks in both pilots were organisational — websites blocked, authentication delays, unclear governance ownership — not technical failure. These can all be planned for.

Trust takes time

It takes a few sessions to get comfortable

Clinicians who trusted the tool early benefited most. Build in support for the first 5–10 uses: peer tips, example prompts, and reassurance help clinicians move from testing to full integration.

Governance and information governance

Establishing the appropriate governance was one of the most time-consuming aspects of the work — not because the governance itself is complex, but because of how it was initiated and prioritised. In one health board, the process involved more than 150 email exchanges and extended over several months.

Where IG and digital teams are given clear backing and permission to prioritise the work, and the right people are brought together early, the core governance activity can be completed in a relatively short period — typically a few days to less than a week of focused effort.

How to do governance well: Set clear organisational priority early. Bring clinical, digital, IG, and procurement stakeholders together from the outset. Work collaboratively rather than via extended email chains. Build on the DPIAs and contractual approaches already completed across health boards — don't start from scratch. Treat governance as an enabler of safe adoption, not a gate to pass through.
05 Recommendations
What comes next

Five things that would make the difference

The combined evidence is now sufficient to move beyond pilot mode. These are the steps that would turn pilot success into system-level change.

  • 1 Move beyond pilot mode — with appropriate support. Active support for the first 5–10 uses for each new clinician, peer-led onboarding, protected time for template iteration, and early IT and IG engagement at each new site. Don't expect instant productivity gains.
  • 2 Keep improving templates with clinicians. Template quality is the single biggest variable in outcome quality. Both pilots confirm that small wording changes can dramatically improve output quality and that complex pathways (e.g. the Autism panel workflow) require more co-design time. Template development should be an ongoing process, not a one-off task.
  • 3 Rethink how services are organised. AI scribing doesn't simply reduce admin time — it changes how a service can function. Consider shorter appointment scheduling, same-day report clearance, faster multi-document workflows, and improved MDT preparation. The capacity gains only materialise if workflows are redesigned to use them.
  • 4 Agree a shared approach to governance across Wales. Variation in IG policies across health boards is a significant barrier to scale. Wales needs a single, standardised position on audio capture — clear rules on storage, deletion, access, and clinical responsibility — so that every health board doesn't have to start from scratch.
  • 5 Track what changes over time. Evaluate beyond per-session time savings: reduced waiting lists, faster decision-making, MDT efficiency, workforce morale and retention, patient and family experience, and equity of access.
