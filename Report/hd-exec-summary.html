Safety and accuracy

The hallucination picture — known, contained, manageable.

The diary study included a dedicated question on hallucinations in every entry. The picture is clear: most use cases are clean, with one area requiring consistent human checking.

✓
Ratification — zero hallucinations
29 consecutive entries. No hallucinations reported in any. 5/5 ease across the board. The most robustly evidenced use case in the study.
⚠
Role / identity errors — low risk
In 2 of 8 Other Uses entries, the clinician's role was mis-stated as "clinical psychologist." All clinical content was correct. Easily verified on review.
!
Medication errors — requires checking
2 of 3 Regular Review entries contained medication hallucinations — one fabricated a "saving medication" statement; one substituted Atomoxetine with Escitalopram. Human review is essential before any medication-related output is used.
Overall hallucination rate: 9 confirmed incidents across 97 entries (approx. 9%). The majority are concentrated in Regular Review and involve medication content — a known risk category for AI scribe tools that requires a clear clinical checking protocol. Ratification, Other Uses (non-role errors) and Clinical Assessment entries show a substantially cleaner picture.
Interpretation

What this means for Hywel Dda.

The pilot data — even mid-study — points to three clear conclusions for senior decision-makers.

01
The time savings are real and material
Across 97 diary entries, clinicians are consistently saving between 26% and 64% of documentation time. For teams under capacity pressure, this is not a marginal gain — it is structural relief.
02
The safety picture is manageable — not hidden
Hallucinations are not absent, but they are understood. The medication errors in Regular Review require a clear checking protocol. Ratification — the highest-volume use case — is clean across 29 consecutive entries.
03
The conditions for scale are now visible
Template development, a medication-checking protocol, and a defined rollout pathway are the remaining requirements. The evidence base to make the case for investment now exists.
