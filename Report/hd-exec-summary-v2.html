Interpretation

What this means for Hywel Dda.

The pilot data — and the voices behind it — point to three clear conclusions.

Finding 01
Magic Notes is already essential for some clinicians
One clinician's statement — "if you take away Magic Notes, there is no way I'll be able to do this" — is not hyperbole. For clinicians managing high ADHD caseloads, the tool has become structural. Removing it would not return the service to a previous state; it would reduce capacity.
Finding 02
The clinical quality impact is as significant as the time saving
Clinicians consistently describe richer reports, captured detail they would otherwise have missed, and more presence in sessions. The 100% quality improvement rating from the ADHD team reflects this. Time saved and quality gained are not in tension — they reinforce each other.
Finding 03
Adoption requires active support — access alone is not enough
The Hywel Dda teams had CTM evidence and peer support, and it still took time and effort to get the tool working well for their specific pathways. Services considering adoption should plan for a supported learning period, iterative template work, and protected time — not expect instant productivity gains.
User needs

What clinicians actually need — and where AI scribe is essential.

From the Welsh ND pathway research. Needs marked as essential are those where AI scribe is not merely useful — it is the most direct, high-confidence way to meet the need.

🎯
Essential needs
AI scribe directly meets this
✓ Focus on the child, not the keyboard. Clinicians can observe and interact fully during assessment rather than splitting attention between the child and notes.
✓ Near-final draft report shortly after the appointment. The most time-consuming part of a clinician's day — report writing — is handled by the tool, not carried home.
✓ Handle multiple speakers, accents and noisy environments. ND assessment rooms are rarely quiet. The tool must be reliable in real clinical conditions, not just ideal ones.
✓ Summarise information from multiple sources. Schools, parents, previous services — clinicians need one coherent view, not five documents open at once.
✓ Maintain clinical tone and professionalism in reports. Clinicians need to be confident signing what the tool produces — not feel they are correcting it.
✓ Capture non-verbal observations and clinical impressions. The tool must allow clinicians to add their own notes quickly after the family leaves — not just transcribe conversation.
✓ Customisable templates that align with local practice. A generic output is not good enough. Templates must iterate easily to match what each service actually produces.
🔬
Still to test at scale
Promising but not yet confirmed
◎ Clear summary of evidence linked to diagnostic criteria for panel decisions
◎ Risk and safeguarding information surfaced before the session starts
◎ Clear recommendations and signposting so families understand next steps
◎ Tailoring outputs for different audiences — family, GP, school, CAMHS — without rewriting

Needs sourced from the Welsh ND pathway research, based on interviews and observations across multiple health boards. Status reflects evidence from the CTM and Hywel Dda pilots.

Scope and limitations

What this tool does — and does not — do.

What it does
✓Records the clinical session with patient consent
✓Produces a structured draft report or summary
✓Frees the clinician to observe, interact and think
✓Captures verbatim quotes and detail that would otherwise be lost
✓Summarises information from uploaded documents
What it does not do
✗Make or recommend diagnoses
✗Make autonomous clinical decisions of any kind
✗Replace clinical judgement or professional responsibility
✗Produce a final document — clinician sign-off is always required
✗Guarantee accuracy — output must be reviewed before use

Step 1 — Hours saved per task, per week

hours/week = (tasks per week × minutes saved) ÷ 60

You enter how many times each task type happens across your whole team per week. This is multiplied by the average minutes Magic Notes saves per task (from the CTM pilot), then divided by 60 to convert to hours. It doesn't matter which clinician does which task — you're entering the team total.

(20 tasks/wk × 30 min ÷ 60) = 10 hrs/wk [New patient assessment (in-person)] (20 tasks/wk × 124.5 min ÷ 60) = 41.5 hrs/wk [Assessment write-up] (20 tasks/wk × 41 min ÷ 60) = 13.7 hrs/wk [Patient summary] (20 tasks/wk × 27.5 min ÷ 60) = 9.2 hrs/wk [ADHD medication review] (40 tasks/wk × 28 min ÷ 60) = 18.7 hrs/wk [Letters & admin]

Step 2 — Total hours over the period

total hours = hours/week × (months × 4.345)

93 hrs/wk × (12 months × 4.345 wks/month) = 93 × 52.1 = 4,849 total hours

4.345 is the average number of weeks in a month (52 ÷ 12).

Step 3 — £ value of time saved

£ value = total hours × hourly rate

4,849 hrs × £50/hr = £242,451

Step 4 — WTE equivalent

WTE = total hours ÷ 1,650

4,849 hrs ÷ 1,650 = 2.94 WTE

1,650 is a standard NHS full-time equivalent annual hours figure (37.5 hrs/wk × 44 working weeks).

Step 5 — Children who could be assessed

children = total hours ÷ 6 → lower estimate (complex cases) children = total hours ÷ 3.5 → upper estimate (straightforward cases)

A full ND assessment typically takes between 3.5 and 6 hours of clinical time, depending on complexity. These are the bounds used to give a range rather than a single figure. This is a rough indicator — not a capacity plan.
